RetroBoard

by Jonas Ekstr√∂m

RetroBoard is a real-time retrospective tool built for remote teams who are tired of clunky, overcomplicated retro software. It focuses on three things: anonymous card submission (so people say what they really think), dot voting (so priorities emerge naturally), and action tracking (so retros actually lead to change). The Slack integration sends a weekly nudge with open action items, keeping accountability alive between sprints.

üöÄ How I Started

Frustration with existing tools

Our team tried 4 different retro tools. All of them were either too complex (Jira), too simple (sticky notes in Miro), or too expensive. I wanted something in between.

Built it during a hackathon

Used a 24-hour company hackathon to build the first version. Real-time sync with WebSockets, anonymous cards, and a simple voting system. The team used it the next day.

Added Slack integration

The biggest problem with retros isn't the meeting ‚Äî it's forgetting the action items. Added a weekly Slack reminder with open items. Completion rate went from 30% to 75%.

Open-sourced it

Put it on GitHub after 3 months. Got 12 stars the first week and a PR from someone adding dark mode. The community feedback loop began.

üí° Tips & Lessons Learned

Build for your own team first

If it solves your problem well, there are 1,000 teams with the same one. Your team is your first focus group, QA team, and customer all in one.

Anonymity changes everything

The moment we made cards anonymous, the quality of feedback skyrocketed. People said what they actually thought instead of what was politically safe.

The follow-up is the product

The retro meeting is 10% of the value. The other 90% is whether action items actually get done. That's why the Slack integration matters more than the board itself.

Hackathons are for validation

Don't use hackathons to build a polished product. Use them to build the ugliest possible thing that answers one question: 'Would my team actually use this?'
