SupplyPulse

by Marco Delgado

SupplyPulse is an internal dashboard built for a mid-size logistics company to visualize supply-chain bottlenecks as they happen. Before SupplyPulse, the team relied on spreadsheets updated twice daily ‚Äî by the time a bottleneck was spotted, it had already cascaded. The dashboard pulls from existing ERP data, applies constraint analysis, and highlights the single biggest blocker at any moment. The result: 40% faster response time and a team that finally speaks the same language about priorities.

Theory of Constraints
Find the one bottleneck, fix it, then find the next one

Most teams try to optimize everything simultaneously and end up improving nothing meaningfully. The Theory of Constraints says your system is only as fast as its slowest point. SupplyPulse makes that slowest point impossible to ignore ‚Äî it's always front and center, glowing red, with the estimated cost of delay.

1
Identify the current constraint (the bottleneck)
2
Exploit it ‚Äî get maximum throughput from it
3
Subordinate everything else to the constraint
4
Elevate ‚Äî invest in removing the constraint
5
Repeat ‚Äî find the new bottleneck

üöÄ How I Started

Observed the pain

Sat with the warehouse team for a week. Watched them alt-tab between 6 spreadsheets to find delays. Realized the data existed ‚Äî it just wasn't connected.

Built a read-only view

Connected to the ERP's API and built a single-screen view of all active orders with status. No editing, no complexity ‚Äî just visibility.

Added the constraint lens

Applied Theory of Constraints thinking: instead of showing everything equally, the dashboard highlights the single biggest blocker and dims everything else.

Rolled out to one team

Deployed to one warehouse team for a 2-week pilot. They caught a supplier delay 6 hours earlier than usual on day 3. That was the proof point.

üí° Tips & Lessons Learned

Talk to the warehouse team first

Domain knowledge beats technical skill every time. I spent a week on the floor before writing a single line of code. That week saved me months of building the wrong thing.

Read-only first

The temptation to build a full CRUD app is strong. Resist it. A read-only dashboard that shows the right data is 10x more valuable than an editable one that shows the wrong data.

Make the bottleneck undeniable

Don't bury insights in charts. Make the most important thing the biggest, brightest element on screen. If someone glances at the dashboard for 2 seconds, they should know the #1 problem.

Sell with time saved, not tech

Leadership didn't care about my API architecture. They cared that response time dropped 40%. Always translate your work into business outcomes.
