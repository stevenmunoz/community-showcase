FocusFlow

by Lena Mori

FocusFlow reimagines the classic Pomodoro technique by making it adaptive. Instead of rigid 25-minute blocks, the app learns your energy patterns throughout the day and adjusts session lengths accordingly. When you're in a high-energy window, sessions stretch to maximize flow state. When energy dips, sessions shorten to maintain momentum without burnout. Built entirely with vanilla JavaScript and localStorage ‚Äî no backend, no accounts, no complexity. Just open the tab and start working.

üß† Mental Model

Parkinson's Law
Work expands to fill the time available

The core insight is simple: if you give yourself 2 hours for a task, it'll take 2 hours. Give yourself 45 minutes and you'll find a way. FocusFlow applies this by dynamically constraining time based on your energy. High energy? You get a longer runway to go deep. Low energy? Short bursts keep you productive without the drag.

1
Observe your energy patterns over a few days
2
Map high/low periods to session lengths
3
Let the constraint drive focus, not willpower
4
Iterate based on completion rates

üöÄ How I Started

Scratched my own itch

I was using a standard Pomodoro app but kept overriding the timer. Some sessions felt too short, others too long. I realized the timer should adapt to me, not the other way around.

Paper prototype

Drew out energy curves on paper and mapped them to timer lengths. Tested the concept manually for a week with phone alarms before writing any code.

Weekend MVP

Built the first version in a weekend using vanilla JS. No frameworks, no build tools. Just an index.html and a script tag. It was ugly but functional.

Shared on Twitter

Posted a screenshot and a short thread about adaptive timers. Got 40 likes and 12 DMs asking to try it. That was enough signal to keep going.

üí° Tips & Lessons Learned

Ship the ugly version first

My v1 was embarrassing ‚Äî Comic Sans header, misaligned buttons, the works. But it taught me what users actually wanted, which had nothing to do with aesthetics.

localStorage is underrated

For solo tools, you don't need a database. localStorage gave me persistence with zero infrastructure. Users loved that there was no sign-up.

Measure completion, not time

I almost built analytics for 'time spent.' Instead I tracked 'sessions completed vs planned.' Way more actionable and motivating for users.

Don't add features, remove friction

Every feature request I got, I asked: 'What friction is this solving?' Half the time, removing a step was better than adding a button.
